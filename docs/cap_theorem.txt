The CAP Theorem

The CAP theorem is a foundational principle in distributed systems. It states that a distributed data store can provide at most two of three guarantees at the same time: consistency, availability, and partition tolerance. Understanding this trade-off helps explain why different databases behave so differently under failure.

Consistency in this context means that every read receives the most recent write or an error; all nodes see the same data at the same time. Availability means that every request receives a non-error response, though not necessarily the most recent data. Partition tolerance means the system continues to operate even when network failures prevent some nodes from communicating with others.

The crucial insight is that network partitions are not optional in a real distributed system. Networks fail, messages are lost, and nodes become unreachable. Because partition tolerance must be assumed, the real choice during a partition is between consistency and availability. When nodes cannot communicate, a system must either refuse some requests to avoid serving stale or conflicting data, preserving consistency, or keep answering requests with potentially outdated data, preserving availability.

Systems are therefore often described as CP or AP. A CP system favors consistency, rejecting requests that it cannot serve correctly during a partition. An AP system favors availability, continuing to respond even at the risk of returning stale data, and reconciling differences once the partition heals.

The theorem is often refined by the related PACELC formulation, which adds that even when there is no partition, a system must trade off between latency and consistency. Strong consistency typically requires coordination across nodes, which adds latency; relaxing consistency reduces it.

In practice many systems offer tunable consistency, letting an application choose per operation how many replicas must respond. This acknowledges that the right balance depends on the use case: a financial ledger leans toward consistency, while a social feed can tolerate eventual consistency in exchange for staying available and fast.
