The National Pension System in India

The National Pension System, or NPS, is a voluntary retirement savings scheme regulated by the Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority of India. It is open to Indian citizens between 18 and 70 years of age and is designed to provide a pension income after retirement through long-term, market-linked investing.

NPS accounts come in two tiers. The Tier I account is the primary retirement account: contributions are locked in until the age of 60, with only limited partial withdrawals allowed for specific purposes such as higher education, marriage, buying a first home, or medical treatment of critical illness. The Tier II account is an optional savings account with no lock-in, but it does not enjoy the same tax benefits.

Money in an NPS account is managed by pension fund managers chosen by the subscriber. Contributions are spread across four asset classes: equity, corporate bonds, government securities, and alternative investment funds. Subscribers can choose Active Choice, where they set their own allocation with equity capped at 75 percent, or Auto Choice, a lifecycle fund where the equity allocation automatically reduces as the subscriber ages.

The tax treatment of NPS is one of its main attractions. Contributions qualify for deduction under Section 80C within the overall limit, and an additional deduction of fifty thousand rupees is available exclusively for NPS under Section 80CCD(1B). At retirement, up to 60 percent of the accumulated corpus can be withdrawn as a tax-free lump sum, while the remaining 40 percent must be used to purchase an annuity that provides a monthly pension, and the annuity income is taxed as regular income.

Charges in NPS are among the lowest of any managed investment product in India, with fund management fees capped at a small fraction of a percent. This low cost structure, combined with decades of compounding, can make a significant difference to the final corpus compared with higher-cost products.

Subscribers can switch fund managers and change asset allocation a limited number of times per year without tax consequences. Performance varies across pension fund managers, so reviewing returns across equity and debt schemes periodically is sensible, while remembering that long-term consistency matters more than short-term rankings.
